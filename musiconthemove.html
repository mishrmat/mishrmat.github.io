music on

the move

1811

music box

The music box was a popular mechanical device in the late 19th century, often used to play simple melodies automatically.

1877

phonograph

Thomas Edison creates the phonograph, a device that records sound and changes the audio and music industries. It worked through mechanical recording and reproduction of sound by using vibration waveforms.

1887

gramophone

Based on the original phonautograph design and created by Emile Berliner, it differed from phonographs due to using a flat spinning disk instead of a spinning cylinder. Gramophones reached the market in 1896 as a Victrola, the first commercially available record player that played disks of recorded music.

1925

record player

Flat disc records being played between 1898 and the late 1950s were played at a speed of approximately 78 revolutions per minute, and this nominal value was chosen as the industry standard.

1954

transistor radio

The Regency TR-1 was the first transistor radio that allowed people to take their music on the go. This handheld, portable music device worked with AM broadcasts.

1963

compact cassette tape

German Austrian engineer Fritz Pfleumer created the magnetic tape by adhering iron oxide power onto thin paper with lacquer. Phillips consolidated the reel-to-reel into the first compact cassette tape, which allowed for music to become more fluid and for mixtapes to be created.

1965

8-track tape

1965 saw recorded music getting into cars with an 8-Track tape. It uses a cartridge containing a looped magnetic tape with eight tracks, hence the name. Ford offered this innovation to be installed in their 1966 models.

1979

walkman

Sony made portable music even more portable with the Walkman, which represented a synergy between the Phillips’ cassette tape and Nathaniel Baldwin’s headphones.

1984

discman

Harnessing surface laser-beam reflections, compact discs converted digital data into analog sound. The invention by Phillips and Sony became an instant threat to cassettes and vinyl. The popular Discman was introduced in 1984 by Sony. This portable CD player, the D-50, entertained many with its portability.

1998

mp3 player

With the development of computer technology, file compression became more sophisticated. Collaboration between German audio engineer Karlheinz Bradenburg and Moving Picture Experts Group (MPEG) with the Internet allowed for a host and standard to be developed. The .MP3 extension was created, and MP3 players became highly popular.

2001

ipod

The original iPod was a groundbreaking MP3 player that could hold 1,000 songs in a pocket-sized device. It featured a 5 GB hard drive, a scroll wheel for navigation, as well as faster data transfer and charging. The iPod was designed to be easy to use and integrated seamlessly with iTunes, making it a popular choice for music lovers.

2005

ipod shuffle

Compact, lightweight, and lacking a display screen, the iPod shuffle was a line of Apple MP3 players known for their small size, affordable price, and focus on shuffling music. They were a popular choice for users who wanted a simple way to listen to music while working out or on the go.

2007

ipod touch

The iPod Touch was the beginning of music devices being much more than just music players. It gave access to the Apple Store, offering features like web browsing, email, and messaging, all while connecting to the internet.

today

the current landscape

With just a few taps, users can explore curated playlists, discover new artists, or revisit their favorite tracks anywhere they go—whether through wireless earbuds, Bluetooth speakers, or in-car audio systems. This convenience has made streaming the dominant way people consume music today.
